Now faith needs a lullaby
There are nights I sit outside and watch the sky do nothing. Just exist. Stars burning themselves out over millions of years and from here it looks like stillness, like peace, like something that has always been and always will be. I used to find that terrifying, the scale of it, the indifference. Now I find myself sitting under it differently. Like maybe the vastness isn't emptiness. Like maybe there's room in it for something new.

We're halfway through this pregnancy, and I still haven't found the right language for what this is doing to me. I keep reaching for words and finding only the shapes of them, hollow casts of something I can't quite hold. Fear doesn't cover it. Joy sounds too clean, too greeting-card, too easy. What I feel is bigger and uglier and more tender than either of those things combined. It sits in my chest like a chord that hasn't resolved yet, hovering in that suspended, aching space between dissonance and something approaching harmony.

I've spent most of my life translating the interior into sound. Pulling feeling out of the dark and pressing it into music because that was the only way I knew how to make it real, make it matter, make it communicate. But this, what's happening now, what's growing in the quiet, this resists translation. It's too alive. Too unfinished. Every time I think I've understood it, it shifts again, becomes something new overnight, like the whole emotional landscape is rearranging itself while I sleep.

I think about my father.

I think about him more than I expected to. More than I probably wanted to, in the way that you don't realise how often someone lives inside you until something seismic happens and suddenly they're everywhere, in your thoughts, in your habits, in the way you reach for a cup of tea at a certain time of morning without knowing why.
He comes back to me in summers, mostly. Long Australian summers that seemed to last forever when I was small, the kind that blur at the edges of memory into something golden and slow. We'd watch cricket together, Test matches that went for days, unhurried and almost ceremonial, the rhythm of them so different from the noise the rest of the world seemed to operate on. Dad would sit in his chair with a beer and I'd lie on the floor with my head propped in my hands and we wouldn't talk much, but it wasn't an uncomfortable silence. It was the comfortable kind. The kind that doesn't need filling. I learned something about patience from those long afternoons. About how some things unfold on their own timeline and the only thing to do is show up and pay attention.

He was a man who showed his love in the showing up. That's what I've come to understand about him, looking back now from this distance. He wasn't a man of grand declarations or easy emotional language — his generation rarely was — but he showed up, consistently and without complaint, in ways that I perhaps didn't fully register as love at the time because they were so quiet about it. So steady.

The tours. God, the tours. When Silverchair started taking us around the world, these kids from Newcastle suddenly standing on stages that seemed impossibly large, in cities that felt like another planet, he came. Not everyone, but more than you'd expect a man with a life and a job and his own responsibilities to manage. He'd appear backstage in some city I'd half-convinced myself I was navigating alone and there he'd be, a little out of place in that world, a little uncertain of what to do with his hands, but there. Present. I used to pretend I didn't need that. I used to perform a kind of independence that wasn't entirely real, because that's what you do at that age, that's the armour you build. But secretly, privately, in the part of me that was still just a kid from a small town who couldn't quite believe any of this was happening, I needed him there. And he knew that, I think, even when I couldn't say it.

He'd watch the shows from the side of the stage sometimes, or from the back of the room, and afterwards he wouldn't say too much. He'd say good show, mate or that song, the third one and then he'd sort of trail off like the words weren't adequate to the thing he'd witnessed, but his face would say the rest. There was a pride in him on those nights that I can still locate if I close my eyes. Not the loud, chest-beating kind. The quiet kind. The kind that lives in the eyes and the set of the jaw. The kind that says I don't entirely understand what you are or how you came from me, but I know it's something, and I am so proud it almost breaks me.

I carried that. I carry it still.

And then there were the sick years.

The years when my body became a kind of battleground, when the weight dropped and the joints ached and getting out of bed felt like an act of extraordinary effort. The years when being in the world felt like too much, and the music that had always been my lifeline started to feel like an obligation I couldn't meet. Those years were frightening in ways I still don't have complete language for. But I was never entirely alone in them.

My father would call. Sometimes just to talk about nothing — the cricket, the neighbours, some minor piece of local news that had no relevance to my life but that tethered me, somehow, to something ordinary. Something outside my own head. And when things got bad enough that nothing could be done from a distance, he'd come. He'd just come. He'd sit with me in whatever state I was in, the difficult, not-easy-to-be-around state of someone who is unwell and frightened and not always capable of being grateful for the care being offered and he wouldn't make me feel like a burden. He wouldn't perform patience. He would simply be patient, which is a different thing entirely and so much harder.

He made tea. He watched whatever was on the television. He read the paper in the same room as me and let the silence be companionable rather than clinical. He didn't push me to be better than I was that day. He met me where I was and stayed there.

I understand now, more than I did then, what that cost him. To watch your child diminish and not be able to fix it must be its own particular agony. He never showed me that agony. He kept it somewhere I couldn't see it and gave me instead the steadiness. The presence. The cup of tea at the right moment.

I owe him more than I've ever said out loud. That's something I'm sitting with now. The particular tenderness of that debt, and the urgency of not letting it go unacknowledged for too much longer.

I think about what I've carried beyond that. The noise inside my head that ran alongside everything else, the way the world used to feel like something pressing down from every direction; relentless, atmospheric, inescapable. The years when coping and not-coping looked almost identical from the outside. The slow, painstaking work of learning to live inside my own skin without wanting to leave it.

But what if I pass something of that on? That question comes for me at 3am, quiet and cold and merciless. What if somewhere in the inheritance, the beautiful parts and the difficult parts, something dark gets handed forward without my meaning it to? What if I look at them one day and see the early signs of something I recognise and don't know how to reach them the way I sometimes couldn't be reached?

And then I think, my father faced a version of that too. He watched me struggle in ways that must have been bewildering to him, that probably had no equivalent in his own experience, and he didn't retreat. He didn't flinch away from the difficulty of me. He loved me through it, steadily and without conditions, with tea and cricket and quiet presence and showing up to cities he'd never heard of to watch his son play music he didn't entirely understand.

If he could do that, if he could love something so far outside his own experience with such consistency and grace, then maybe I can too. Maybe that's the most important inheritance. Not the darkness. The endurance.

And then there's Jade.

There's a version of this story where I talk around her, where she exists in the periphery as context, as backdrop. But that would be dishonest, and we've moved too far past dishonesty to go back now.

What's happening between us, the shift that started the moment we found out, it's something I don't have clean language for either. It's not that we've become different people. It's more like we've become more of ourselves, but in the direction of each other. Like two rivers that have been running parallel finally finding the point where the land gives way and they become the same body of water.

I've loved her for a long time. But loving someone and building something with someone, really building, foundation and all, those are different acts. And I don't think I fully understood that until now. Until I watched her move through the world carrying this extraordinary thing, this new life, and realised that she is doing something I will never fully comprehend with her body, her energy, her entire being. There's an intimacy in witnessing that which goes beyond anything I thought I understood about closeness.

There are moments when I look at her and feel something I can only describe as awe. Not the performative kind. The quiet, private kind that you don't say out loud because it's too fragile, too real. She's remarkable in ways I keep discovering, even now. Especially now. The way she holds her own fears without letting them capsize her. The way she can be soft and grounded in the same breath. I've always been more prone to extremes, all the way under or trying desperately to get above the surface. She finds the middle. She always has. I used to think that was a kind of steadiness I didn't need. Now I understand it's the thing that might save me. Save us.

But I'd be lying if I said it wasn't complicated too. Because when something this significant enters a relationship, it changes the geometry of it. The space between two people rearranges itself. And sometimes I lie awake next to her and wonder if we're growing together through this or if there's some invisible distance opening that neither of us is naming yet. I think that's just the fear talking. The part of me that has always, on some level, been bracing for the moment love decides I'm too much, or not enough, or something in between. I'm trying to learn to interrupt that pattern in real time. To notice it and name it and set it down before it becomes a self-fulfilling thing.

She helps with that. She doesn't always know she's doing it. But she does.

What I want to give this child, what I am determined to give them is everything my father gave me, and then the things that were harder for him. I want to give them the long summer afternoons of comfortable silence, the showing up without being asked, the steadiness in the difficult seasons. I want to sit with them through their sick days and their hard years and their bewildering triumphs, just as he sat with me. I want to be in the back of whatever room they're standing in, watching with that quiet pride that doesn't need to announce itself.

But I also want to give them the words he sometimes couldn't find. The direct ones. The ones that say I see you and I'm proud of you and whatever you are, you came from me and I think you're extraordinary said plainly, said often, not left to be inferred from presence alone. Because presence is everything, and my father gave me that, and it was more than enough to survive on. But I want them to have more than survival. I want them to have the full thing.

I want to understand their frequency from the beginning. Not wait until they've spent years broadcasting into what feels like empty air and then try to tune in. I want to be already listening, before they have words, before they have a way of telling me what they need, reading them the way you learn to read music, slowly and with full attention, until it becomes instinct.

My father loved me with everything he had. I believe that without reservation. And what he had was considerable, more than I sometimes gave him credit for when I was younger and more certain about what love was supposed to look like. I understand now that love takes as many forms as there are people doing the loving, and his form was steady and unglamorous and deeply, quietly real. He gave me roots when I was trying to become something rootless and atmospheric. He kept me tethered to something solid when everything else was noise.

I want to be that for this child. And I want to be more. Not because he wasn't enough because he was, he was more than enough but because he gave me a foundation strong enough to build on. That's what you do with a good inheritance. You don't waste it. You take it further.

When I pressed my hand to her stomach and felt that small, impossible movement — that kick, that declaration of existence — something cracked open in me. Not healed. Not resolved. Just open. Like a window in a room that's been sealed for too long and suddenly the air is different, almost too much all at once, and you have to decide whether to step back or lean in.

I leaned in.

I laid there and I cried the ugly, surprised kind of crying that comes from somewhere you didn't know was full. And in that moment, just for a breath, I thought of my father watching me from the side of a stage in some city I can't even remember now. I thought of him sitting in his chair on a long summer afternoon, beer in hand, not needing to say anything because his presence was already saying everything. I thought of him on the other end of a phone call, or in a chair beside my bed, or in the back of a room full of noise and light, watching his son with that quiet, breaking pride.

And I understood, maybe for the first time, fully, what all of that cost him and what it meant. What it means. This is what it is, I thought. This is exactly what it is. I'm not ready. I've made peace with that. Readiness is a myth we tell ourselves about the small things. The big things arrive before you're prepared, and you meet them with whatever you've managed to become up until that point.

What I've managed to become, up until this point, is my father's son. And I mean that now as the most complete compliment I know how to give to him, and to myself.

Halfway through.

We're halfway through, and I am more afraid and more certain and more grateful than I have ever been about anything in my life. More in love than I ever have been.

And I am exactly where I want to be.
